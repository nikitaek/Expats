## Scope and method

This scan focuses on the public web footprint of the operators from your priority list for **Da Nang, Hoi An, and the Hoiana / Nam Hoi An corridor** as of **June 5, 2026**. I treated publicly visible hotel pages, hotel search pages, and live package-result pages as evidence that an operator is actively selling that hotel into the Russian-speaking market. I did **not** treat generic destination articles or sightseeing itineraries alone as strong hotel-placement evidence. That matters because some operators in your list surfaced real hotel inventory, while others mostly surfaced combined tours or informational pages. ## Caveats that matter for mapping

This is a **supply-side proxy**, not a nationality report. A hotel being sold by many Russian-speaking operators does **not** prove its guest mix is majority Russian. It does mean that the hotel is **easy to place into Russian-speaking package demand**, which is the best public signal available short of on-the-ground guest-counting. The strongest predictive combination is therefore **operator overlap + mid-market pricing + repeated presence on major package operators**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search0Ņłéturn15search3Ņłéturn12search3Ņłéturn10search0Ņłü

There are also real public-web blind spots. Some operators in your list expose much deeper inventory inside agent-only systems or dynamic booking engines than search engines reveal. That is why operators such as **Intourist**, **Spectrum**, and **TourTrans** looked weaker on hotel-level visibility in this scan, even though they may still move Russian-speaking travellers through the corridor. Likewise, some niche operators exposed **USD no-flight hotel rates** rather than ruble package prices, which is why the table uses a normalized segment instead of pretending all visible rates are directly comparable.
